Abstract

Recent advances in imitation learning have enabled robots to execute an increasingly diverse set of manipulation skills. However, real-world manufacturing assembly and disassembly require long-horizon, multi-task operation, where robots must identify and switch between context-appropriate skills. A single scene may contain multiple task objectives—such as BNC, terminal block, USB, and other connectors—requiring the robot to reason over several possible goals within the same observation space. Moreover, each connector type demands distinct motion strategies (e.g., wiggling, prying, rotating to unlock), making it difficult for policies trained solely on visual and force observations to reliably generalize. Humans naturally overcome this complexity by segmenting long-horizon demonstrations into meaningful sub-task modes and using high-level reasoning, often expressed in language, to guide sequencing and execution. Motivated by this insight, we tackle two key challenges: (1) improving policy efficiency and robustness by structuring manipulation into language-guided sub-task modes, and (2) enabling a high-level Vision-Language Model (VLM) to select the appropriate sub-task based on natural-language prompts and scene context. Furthermore, we introduce an adaptive modality-weighting framework that dynamically adjusts the contribution of visual and force cues depending on the current sub-task mode.
